The Concept

A fully automated content production pipeline. AI pulls Brett's 14+ news sources via RSS, writes cited summaries, generates a script tuned to Brett's voice, and handles all post-production and multi-platform distribution. Brett's only job is to review and record, which takes roughly 30–45 minutes.

Who it's for: Brett (the creator). This solves his production bottleneck, not the audience's content discovery problem.

User Journey

1
Source Ingestion AI scans Brett's subscribed sources every morning and pulls the top stories by relevance and timeliness.
2
Summarization AI writes cited summaries with key facts extracted. Factual claims only, no verbatim lifting.
3
Script Generation AI drafts a full episode script matched to Brett's editorial voice and preferred structure.
Brett's active time starts here — ~30–45 min total
4
Review & Record Brett reviews the script, makes edits, and records audio. This is the only step that actually needs him.
5
Production & Distribution AI handles editing, section markers, clip generation, metadata, and upload to all platforms.

What Makes It Different — Honestly

This is the obvious solution. Multiple AI podcast tools already do something similar. What sets this apart is execution quality: the n8n + multi-model stack working together, and the hard constraint of under 60 minutes end-to-end. The new thing is how it all fits together and the speed discipline, not the format itself.

Why This Matters + Scorecard

This concept does exactly what Brett asked for: cut the time from reading to published episode. It's the pragmatic, build-it-now option. But there's a real gap here. It produces content without answering why anyone would listen.

Impact
3/5

Solves Brett's production problem but says nothing about audience growth or what makes this different. High value for Brett personally, unclear value for listeners.

Innovation
2/5

Straight implementation of existing AI podcast tools. Other platforms already do similar things. No moat. The Washington Post tried this and reported a 67% failure rate.

Feasibility
5/5

Most technically straightforward of the three concepts. Everything needed exists today. We could have a working prototype in Sprint 1 at ~$120/mo.

Business Impact
3/5

Cuts Brett's time per episode significantly, but doesn't build anything defensible. Monetization depends on podcast ad revenue in a crowded market where nothing stands out.

Overall Score 3.25 / 5

Core tension: This concept speeds up production but doesn't answer the mentor's central question: why would anyone listen to this over other AI-generated content? Being fast to produce is not a reason for someone to hit play.

Next Steps

Riskiest Assumption (RAT)

Can AI-generated scripts get close enough to Brett's editorial voice that he's not rewriting most of the content?

2-Week Experiment

Run one real episode through the full pipeline, from source ingestion to published, and measure actual time and output quality.

Go Threshold

Brett's review time < 15 min. Quality acceptable for publish without major rewrites.

No-Go Threshold

Brett rewrites > 50% of script. Total time from source to publish exceeds 60 min.

Missing Information

  • Brett's full source list (promised in kickoff, not yet received)
  • Brett's voice recording so the AI can learn his style
  • Legal review of the content licensing approach for automated summarization

Client Decisions Needed

  • Does Brett accept AI-assisted scripts, or does he prefer fully unscripted recording?
  • Which distribution platforms are priority (Apple, Spotify, YouTube, RSS)?

Risk to flag: This concept solves production but NOT differentiation. Even a perfectly automated pipeline puts out content that competes in a market where everything sounds the same. The question "why would anyone listen?" is still open and needs to be answered, either here or in Concepts 2 and 3.
